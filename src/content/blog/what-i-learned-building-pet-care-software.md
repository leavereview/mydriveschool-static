I spent seven years building RevelationPets.com — a management platform for pet care businesses. Kennels, catteries, doggy daycares, grooming salons. By the time a buyer approached me in 2022, the platform was serving over 1,000 paying customers across five countries.

That experience taught me more about what service businesses actually need from software than any degree ever could. And it's the reason DriveSchoolPro exists today.

This is the story of what I built, what I learned, and why I believe driving schools are facing the exact same problems pet care businesses were struggling with a decade ago.

## How RevelationPets Started

RevelationPets wasn't a side project that grew legs. It was deliberate from day one. I'd studied Software Engineering at the University of Winchester — graduated with First Class Honours — and I wanted to build something that solved a real, specific problem for a real, specific group of people.

Pet care businesses were it. The industry was growing fast, owners were juggling bookings across spreadsheets, paper diaries, and Post-it notes, and the existing software options were either clunky desktop tools or wildly overpriced for what they offered. I saw an opportunity to build something modern, simple, and affordable.

So I did. I started solo — designing, coding, and supporting every customer myself.

## Scaling to 1,000 Customers

The early days were exactly what you'd expect. Building features, answering support emails at midnight, and learning that what I thought customers wanted and what they actually wanted were often completely different things.

The number one pain point, by a mile, was **scheduling and bookings**. Every pet care business I spoke to was drowning in it. Double-bookings, missed appointments, phone tag with clients trying to confirm drop-off times. The scheduling problem was universal — it didn't matter whether they were a two-person cattery in Devon or a 30-kennel operation in Auckland.

So I built the best scheduling system I could. And then rebuilt it. And rebuilt it again, because every time I thought I'd nailed it, customers would show me an edge case I'd never considered.

Over time, I hired help and the platform grew. We expanded to the UK, US, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand. By the time we hit 1,000 customers, I'd learned some hard lessons about what it actually takes to build software that service businesses rely on every day.

## The Three Things I Learned the Hard Way

### 1. Customer Support Is the Product

This is the biggest lesson, and it took me far too long to fully absorb it.

When you're building software for service business owners — people who are hands-on, busy, and often not particularly technical — the quality of your support is inseparable from the quality of your product. A feature that's technically brilliant but confusing to set up is a bad feature. A bug that takes three days to fix costs your customer real money in missed bookings or confused clients.

At RevelationPets, I learned that the fastest way to improve the product was to answer every support ticket personally. Not because I was being heroic, but because every ticket was a signal. If three people asked the same question in a week, the interface was wrong. If someone couldn't figure out how to set up automated reminders, the onboarding flow needed rethinking.

By the time I sold the business, I'd built a support operation that was fast, personal, and deeply integrated with how we prioritised development. That approach is baked into DriveSchoolPro from day one.

### 2. Easy Software Gets Used — Clever Software Gets Abandoned

I built features at RevelationPets that I was genuinely proud of from an engineering perspective. Clever reporting dashboards, flexible configuration options, powerful filtering tools. Some of them were barely touched.

The features that customers loved — the ones they told their friends about, the ones that actually reduced their admin time — were almost always the simplest ones. One-click booking confirmations. Automated reminders that just worked out of the box. A calendar view that showed the whole week without needing a manual.

I learned that reducing the learning curve isn't just a nice-to-have — it's the difference between software that gets adopted and software that gets trialled for a week and abandoned. If someone can't figure out your tool in ten minutes, they're going back to their paper diary. And honestly, you can't blame them.

This lesson shapes every design decision in DriveSchoolPro. The goal isn't to build the most powerful driving school software. It's to build the one that a driving instructor can set up between lessons and actually use every day without thinking about it.

### 3. Every Service Business Has the Same Core Problems

Across 1,000 pet care businesses in five countries, problems were remarkably consistent. Scheduling chaos. No-shows eating into revenue. Clients who couldn't easily see their upcoming bookings. Staff who needed to pick up where someone else left off. Payments that were tracked on paper or not tracked at all.

The specific details varied — a kennel worries about feeding schedules while a groomer worries about breed-specific notes — but the structural problems were identical. And after seven years of solving those problems for pet care, I started noticing them everywhere.

Including in driving schools.

## Why Driving Schools?

The honest answer is that it started with my own children's driving instructor.

He was a good instructor — experienced, patient, professional. But his entire business ran through WhatsApp. Booking a lesson meant sending a message and waiting for a reply. Confirming a time meant another round of messages. Rescheduling was a thread of back-and-forth that could take a day to resolve. There was no way to see upcoming lessons, no record of what had been covered in previous sessions.

I recognised the pattern immediately. This was the same problem I'd spent seven years solving for pet care businesses — just in a different uniform.

So I started researching. I talked to instructors. I looked at what tools were available. And what I found was striking.

### Paper Diaries and WhatsApp Groups

The vast majority of UK driving instructors — including many running multi-instructor schools — were managing their entire operation with a combination of paper diaries, WhatsApp messages, and maybe a basic Google Calendar. There was no centralised system. No automated reminders. No way for students to check their own progress or upcoming lessons.

The tools that did exist were either generic booking platforms that didn't understand the specific needs of driving instruction, or legacy software that looked and felt like it was built fifteen years ago. Nothing tracked DVSA competencies properly. Nothing offered the kind of simple, mobile-first experience that instructors could realistically use between pickups.

### The DVSA Gap

This was the clincher. The DVSA's [Ready to Pass framework](https://driveschoolpro.com/dvsa-27-driving-skills/) sets out 27 specific driving skills across 8 categories that every learner needs to master before they're genuinely test-ready. It's the official standard — the same framework examiners use.

But none of the existing tools tracked it properly. At best, you'd get a free-text notes field or a basic checkbox. Nothing that mapped the full 27 skills with proper proficiency levels. Nothing that let multiple instructors see where a student was up to. Nothing that let the student themselves see their own progress.

For someone who'd spent seven years building competency tracking for pet care businesses — where you need to know each animal's vaccination status, dietary requirements, and behavioural notes across multiple staff members — this felt like familiar territory. The domain was different, but the data problem was the same: structured information that multiple people need to see, update, and trust.

## What I Carried Over

DriveSchoolPro isn't RevelationPets with a different logo. The driving instruction industry has its own specific needs, regulations, and workflows. But the principles I learned building software for 1,000 pet care businesses are embedded in everything I've built.

**Automated reminders that work out of the box.** At RevelationPets, automated reminders were the single most impactful feature we built. Customers told us they cut no-shows by half or more. DriveSchoolPro has the same approach — configurable email reminders with one-click confirmation, because a missed driving lesson costs the instructor real money. [Read more about reducing no-shows →](https://driveschoolpro.com/blog/reduce-no-shows-driving-school/)

**Scheduling that prevents conflicts, not just displays them.** The biggest lesson from pet care scheduling was that showing a conflict after it's been created is almost useless. You need to prevent it at the point of booking. DriveSchoolPro checks student, instructor, and vehicle availability simultaneously — [three-way conflict detection](https://driveschoolpro.com/driving-school-scheduling-software/) — before a booking is confirmed.

**A student portal that reduces phone calls.** Pet care clients constantly rang to check their booking details, even when they'd received a confirmation email. Giving them a simple self-service portal — where they could see upcoming appointments and account balances — dramatically reduced inbound enquiries. DriveSchoolPro's [student portal](https://driveschoolpro.com/driving-school-management-software/) does the same thing, plus it shows DVSA progress so students can see exactly where they are across all 27 skills.

**Software that's simple enough to use between appointments.** Pet care staff checked the system between animal drop-offs and pickups. Driving instructors check between lessons, often from their phone while parked up. If the interface requires a training session to get started, it's failed before it's started.

## What's Different

The obvious difference is the [DVSA framework](https://driveschoolpro.com/dvsa-27-driving-skills/). Pet care has vaccination records and feeding schedules. Driving instruction has a structured, government-defined competency framework that every learner needs to progress through. Building proper tracking for 27 skills across 8 categories with 6 proficiency levels — and making it fast enough that an instructor can update it in under a minute after each lesson — was a new challenge. But it's the feature that makes DriveSchoolPro genuinely different from anything else on the market.

The other difference is focus. RevelationPets grew to serve multiple countries, and that brought complexity — different regulations, different expectations, different support hours. DriveSchoolPro is built specifically for UK ADIs. The DVSA framework, the terminology, the pricing, the design decisions — everything is focused on how driving instruction works in England, Scotland, and Wales. That focus means fewer compromises and a better product for the people who actually use it.

## The Pattern I Keep Seeing

After building software for two different service industries, I'm convinced the pattern is universal. Service businesses — whether they're looking after dogs, teaching people to drive, or running a tattoo studio — share the same structural challenges. Scheduling is chaos. No-shows eat margins. Client communication is manual and fragmented. Progress tracking (where applicable) is inconsistent. And the people running these businesses are too busy actually delivering the service to spend hours learning complicated software.

The opportunity is always the same: build something simple, specific, and genuinely useful. Solve the scheduling problem first, because that's where the pain is sharpest. Automate reminders, because that's where the ROI is most obvious. And make it easy enough that someone can set it up in ten minutes and never look back.

That's what I built for pet care. That's what I'm building for driving schools. And after seven years and 1,000 customers the first time around, I'm fairly confident about what works.
